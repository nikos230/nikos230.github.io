Diploma (Integrated Master) Thesis

Title: Data-driven Wildfire Spread Prediction using Deep Learning

Abstract

This project aims to predict final burned area from a wildfire as it is essential for effective risk management, emergency response, and strategic planning. This theesis approachs wildfire spread prediction as a machine learning task, utilizing historical Earth observation data from 2006 to 2022 to estimate the final burned area resulting from wildfire events. To that end a spatio-temporal dataset is created consisting of remote sensing data, including meteorological observations, vegetation maps, land cover, topography, and thermal anomalies. Implementing a variety of deep learning models trained on different temporal windows to capture the spatio-temporal context of fire spread, and compared them to a baseline model trained only on ignition-day data. Findings suggest that incorporating forecast days into the models enables more accurate predictions, with the best-performing model achieving an average F1 score of 57.2% and an Intersection over Union of 41.9%. Add data and models are open-source.
